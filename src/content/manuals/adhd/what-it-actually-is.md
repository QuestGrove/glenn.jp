ADHD stands for Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder. The name is misleading on almost every word.

There is no deficit of attention. There is a difference in *how* attention is regulated. People with ADHD can focus intensely — sometimes more intensely than anyone else in the room — on things that engage them. The problem is that the engagement isn't voluntary in the same way.

The hyperactivity is often internal. Many adults with ADHD, especially those diagnosed late, don't present as visibly restless. The hyperactivity lives in the mind: a constant background hum of thoughts, an impatience that has nowhere to go.

## What's actually different

The core difference is in the regulation of attention, emotion, and impulse. Not a lack of these — a difference in how consistently they can be directed.

Think of it like a spotlight versus a floodlight. Most people have a spotlight they can aim. People with ADHD have a floodlight that illuminates everything at once and is very hard to narrow. The floodlight is not worse — it sees things the spotlight misses. But in a world built for spotlights, it creates friction.

## Why diagnosis often comes late

The coping strategies that intelligent people develop can mask ADHD for a very long time. You learn to work harder, stay up later, rely on adrenaline from deadlines. It works — until it doesn't.

For many people the unmasking happens in adulthood when the scaffolding of school and routine disappears and they have to self-direct entirely.

## A useful reframe

ADHD is not a character flaw. It is not laziness, not lack of willpower, not immaturity. It is a neurological difference that has real costs in certain environments and real advantages in others.

Understanding this is not an excuse to stop trying. It is the foundation for trying *effectively*, which is different from trying harder at the wrong things.
